    U.S. Constitution

    Article I: Powers of Congress

    Article 1:

    Article I of the Constitution of the United States establishes the legislative branch of the federal government, the United States Congress. Under Article I, Congress is a bicameral legislature consisting of the House of Representatives and the Senate. Article I defines the structure, powers, and procedures of the legislative branch of the federal government. It is a foundational part of the Constitution's system of checks and balances between the three branches of government.

    Sections of Article 1:

    Section 1
    • Establishes the legislative branch of the federal government, known as the United States Congress.
    • Congress consists of a Senate and a House of Representatives.
    Section 2
    • Lays out the composition and election of the House of Representatives.
    • Members of the House are elected every two years and seats are apportioned based on state population.
    Section 3
    • Establishes the Senate, with two senators from each state.
    • Originally, senators were elected by state legislatures, until the 17th Amendment provided for direct election by the people.
    Section 4
    • Grants states the power to regulate the congressional election process.
    • Requires Congress to assemble at least once per year.
    Section 5
    • Lays out various rules for both houses of Congress.
    • Grants each chamber the power to determine the qualifications of its own members and punish or expel members.
    Section 6
    • Establishes the compensation, privileges, and restrictions of those holding congressional office.
    Section 7
    • Lays out the procedures for passing a bill, including the president's veto power and Congress' ability to override a veto.
    Section 8
    • Grants Congress various enumerated powers, including the power to tax, borrow money, regulate commerce, declare war, raise armies, and more.
    Section 9
    • Places various limits on the powers of Congress.
    Section 10
    • Prohibits states from certain actions, such as coining money or declaring war.

    History of Senate and the House of Representatives:

    The current structure of the U.S. Congress, with a bicameral legislature consisting of a Senate and House of Representatives, that was established through the drafting and ratification of the U.S. Constitution in 1787-1788. The Great Compromise was what created both of the Senate and the House of Representative. The Great Compromise was a compromise between the large states and the small states over how they would be represented in the congress. The large states wanted representation in Congress to be proportional to population, as proposed in the Virginia Plan. The small states wanted equal representation for all states, as proposed in the New Jersey Plan. The Great Compromise, was proposed by Roger Sherman and Oliver Ellsworth saying that the House of Representatives would have representation proportional to each state's population, meanwhile the Senate would have equal representation, with two senators per state.
